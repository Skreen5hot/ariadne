# Notes from Inside the Construction Site

### A Response to *Magnifica Humanitas*

*A response to the Encyclical Letter* Magnifica Humanitas *of His Holiness Leo XIV (15 May 2026). Paragraph references are to the encyclical.*

-----

*Magnifica Humanitas* asks to be read by those who build, not only by those who watch the building. Its §111 turns directly to developers: they bear “a particular ethical and spiritual responsibility, for every design choice reflects a vision of humanity.” What follows is one builder’s attempt to answer the encyclical on the terms it sets — to take the kind of implementation it makes morally unavoidable and ask what, concretely, it requires of a foundation. It is offered not to dispute the teaching but to work beneath it.

A short orientation first, because the rest depends on it. The encyclical’s concerns sort into three levels, and an honest builder should say at the outset which ones a machine can touch:

1. **Substrate** — opacity, provenance, reversibility, attribution: whether a system can be inspected and held to account at all.
1. **Governance** — who decides, who participates, who contests, who benefits.
1. **The anthropological limit** — conscience, grace, embodiment, moral responsibility, personhood.

Architecture speaks strongly to the first, partially to the second, and to the third only by way of refusal. The controlling claim of this response is that distinction: a build can dissolve the *dangers* the encyclical enumerates, but it does not touch the *negation* at the heart of §99. Keeping those two apart is the whole discipline.

## 1. The negation comes first

Before any claim about what a build can do, the thing it cannot do. §99 holds that these systems possess no moral conscience — that they do not judge good and evil, do not grasp the ultimate meaning of a situation, do not bear responsibility. I read this not as a safety finding but as an anthropological one, and I accept it as a boundary rather than a problem to be solved. Years earlier, in a paper I called *A Line in the Sand*, I argued the same boundary from the other side: that consciousness is non-commodifiable — it cannot be owned, manufactured, or traded — and that treating it as a product is the category error from which the downstream harms follow. No architecture I can describe bridges that gap. The rest of this response stands on the near side of it: whatever a build improves, it improves at the first two levels; the third it can only decline to violate.

## 2. The builder’s mood

The encyclical moves, by its nature, in the prophetic and evaluative mood. It names what AI cannot be, and from that lack derives a catalogue of dangers, met largely by oversight applied from outside the build — regulation, independent review, a deliberately slower pace (§106). This is right and necessary. But it is not the only place to stand. The same catalogue, read from inside, is a requirements document. Opacity, delegated judgment, simulated intimacy, surveillance, exclusion-by-algorithm, concentration of power — each is a property some foundation will settle one way or another, whether or not anyone chooses deliberately.

The central claim has to be stated carefully, because the strong form is false. A build cannot *foreclose* these dangers. Pride, domination, and the technocratic vision the encyclical warns against — its caution that the real threat is an anti-human equation of fullness of life with control, optimization, and the elimination of uncertainty — are not abolished by good engineering. What a build can do is amplify or conceal them, or else constrain, expose, and structurally resist them. My claim is not that architecture abolishes the moral problem. It is that bad architecture silently decides it in advance, and that the decision, once embedded, becomes invisible. The work is to drag that decision into the open — to make it explicit, contestable, and reversible rather than hidden.

## 3. Three implementation claims

If the dangers are design constraints, here is what answering them looks like at each level a build can actually reach.

**Transparency of the moral frame.** The encyclical’s sharpest structural warning is §107: a merely “aligned” system still leaves the operative morality in the hands of a few, who become “the invisible infrastructure” of the thing. My answer is a protocol, ARCHON, whose function I want to state before its machinery. ARCHON is not meant to replace the Church’s own moral reasoning — natural law, prudence, conscience, ecclesial discernment. It is an attempt to make whatever moral framework is actually operating explicit, inspectable, and revisable, so that no hidden anthropology is smuggled into the system under the name of neutrality. Beneath it sits the deliberative engine I have built — Integral Ethics, a process that reasons across a plurality of worldviews rather than a single concealed one, worked out in *Beyond the Categorical Imperative* and *Toward a Realist Ontology of Value*. That engine is itself contestable, and I will say below exactly where it is unproven. The point at this level is only that the frame is forced into the open, where §104 and §107 insist it belong.

**Auditability and contestability.** §98 worries that current systems are “more cultivated than built,” their inner workings unknown; §105 asks that someone be identifiable who must account for a decision, justify it, monitor it, and contest it. My substrate is a direct reply at the level it can reach. The orchestrator is deterministic; its state is SHA-256 hash-chained; every action is written to an audit log that cannot be silently revised; identity is content-addressed through HIRI, so that what was decided, on what basis, and by whose authorship is fixed rather than asserted after the fact. The Triple-I standard — Irreversibility, Inseparability, Integrity-Maintenance — formalizes the same instinct. None of this makes the system moral. It makes the system *legible*, which is the precondition for anyone outside it to hold it to account.

**Subsidiarity and edge-first design.** Against the concentration of power the encyclical returns to (§71–72, §108–109), my Edge-Canonical First principle and the Sovereign Personal Compute Node keep data and compute with the person: no required central infrastructure, offline-first. Where the encyclical asks that data be treated as a shared good rather than appropriated by a few, the aim is to make appropriation difficult to perform rather than merely wrong to perform.

And here the most important correction to my own posture. None of these is a substitute for external governance, and I do not want the energy of an inside-out argument to imply otherwise. *Magnifica Humanitas* does not merely call for better design; it calls for participation, subsidiarity, communities able to share in discernment and oversight, real ownership of data, mechanisms that keep AI power from resting in a few hands. External accountability is part of the moral form of the build, not a patch applied after it. An inside-out architecture is not a replacement for law, ecclesial discernment, public accountability, worker voice, or democratic participation. It is the technical precondition that lets those forms of accountability become real rather than theatrical — because contestability the substrate makes impossible is contestability no regulation can later supply.

## 4. What the architecture cannot reach

This returns to where it began. The substrate and governance work address levels one and two; level three it can only respect from a distance, and honesty requires naming the gaps rather than letting architectural completeness pose as anthropological completeness.

The negation itself is untouched. Conscience, the bearing of responsibility, the capacity to grasp the ultimate meaning of a situation — these are not failure modes to design against. My “factory of faculties” framing is meant to keep me on the near side of §99 by producing components of moral reasoning rather than a moral subject. But that this framing is *stable under composition* — that faculties can be assembled arbitrarily close together without crossing into a personhood claim — is an assertion I cannot prove, and may be the deepest open problem in the program.

The grace-completed anthropology lies wholly outside it. The encyclical’s teaching that the human flourishes *through* limit and finitude (§118–122), and that authentic self-transcendence comes by grace rather than technical divinization (§127–128), names categories no deterministic substrate touches. I do not claim to synthesize them; I claim only not to counterfeit them.

The deliberative engine’s deepest seam is unreconciled. Integral Ethics draws on a plurality of worldviews whose compatibility with the encyclical’s Thomistic and Augustinian frame I have asserted more than demonstrated. A careful reader is entitled to treat that as the program’s softest joint, and I would not argue.

ARCHON faces its own §107 critique, turned inward. Whoever sets the frame governs, and the question of who authorizes the framework — and by what accountable process it is itself open to correction — I have specified more thoroughly than I have solved. Pluralism of design does not by itself answer it.

Contestability is not responsibility. The hash chain attributes every decision to a human author and forbids secret revision; it answers §105 in the epistemic register only. It relocates responsibility cleanly to persons, which is correct, but it must never be presented as though auditability discharged the moral accountability the encyclical means.

And most of this is specification, demonstrated in containment, not at scale. A first autonomous contained-loop proof shows the substrate behaves as designed in a bounded setting; it does not show the moral architecture holds under deployment, adversarial pressure, or the slow corrosions the encyclical rightly fears. The central target remains undemonstrated, and a sober reader should treat it so until it is not.

## 5. One builder’s wall

This is offered, then, not as *the* implementation of the encyclical. A magisterial document does not “stop short” of code; it operates at the level of moral, theological, and social discernment, and is complete there. It is offered as one builder’s attempt to take seriously the kind of implementation *Magnifica Humanitas* makes morally unavoidable. The encyclical asks whether a given technology makes human life “more human… more worthy of man” (§129). I cannot answer that with proof for the work described here. I can say only that the question has been the design constraint from the first stone, and that the foundation has been laid so the answer cannot later be quietly evaded. If even the contingent dangers can be made harder to hide, harder to deny, and harder to impose, then the construction site of our time can be, here and there, a little more Jerusalem than Babel — and where it cannot, the gap is better named in the open than mortared over. Nehemiah’s wall was raised by many distributed hands, relationship before stone. This is one of them.
